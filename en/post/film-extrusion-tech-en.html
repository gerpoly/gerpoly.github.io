Raising Standards, Advances in Film Extrusion Technology

Posted by Gerpoly
2017-10-28   Film Extrusion

  Manufacturers of extrusion lines are under pressure. Their customers are demanding faster, more sophisticated lines - with closer control, faster line speed and lower energy consumption.
  Thankfully for all concerned, manufacturers and researchers are engaged at the cutting edge, helping to speed up production, process new materials and boost energy efficiency.
  Windmoller & Holscher says it has developed a blown film line that slashes energy costs - by targeting the cooling process.
  ”Energy consumption during the melting process is a physical constant, and there is only minor potential for improvement,” said Torsten Schmitz, head of cast film extrusion lines at W&H.
  ”In contrast, cooling technology has the greatest potential for optimization.”
  He said that incorporating intelligent, automated cooling technology into its Filmex II cast film extrusion line can save up to 70% in energy costs.
  The approach combines a free cooler, a system that uses low external air temperatures, and a chiller. It is controlled centrally via the line’s Procontrol operator panel. An automatic mode always selects the optimum operation.
  A free cooler requires less than one-fifth of the energy of a chiller. The challenge is that a free cooler can only be used when the outdoor temperature is lower than the required process temperature. Until now, this was the limiting factor.
  ”The casting roll is generally run at a higher temperature than the chill roll for both stretch and barrier films,” said Schmitz. “These different requirements could not be addressed separately because one circuit supplies all of the elements. This resulted in unnecessary cooling, wasted energy and additional costs.”
  The new approach resolves the problem by using a second cooling circuit. This allows the system to handle different process temperatures.
  For normal stretch film production, the casting roller can be cooled entirely using the free cooler - even at an outdoor temperature of 30°C. This more than halves the work done by the chiller. At outdoor temperatures up to 1 0°C, the system is cooled using combinations of the free cooler and the chiller. At temperatures below 1 0°C, the free cooler handles all the cooling.
  ”Investment in the additional cooling circuit and a free cooler pays itself off for a stretch film system in central Europe after around one year,” said Schmitz.” We have not even factored in the added value of sustainability which can be used as an extra sales point.”
  W&H says that, by offering the intelligent cooling circuit, it has continued to develop the capabilities of the Filmex II as an ‘intelligent machine’.

Flat performance
  Meanwhile, Reifenhauser says it can help blown film extruders to cope with wave phenomena and surfaces that are not perfectly plane.
  ”Stiffer film containing PIDPE or PP are more affected by these problems, which frequently also occur in the production of barrier films,” said the company. “Our Evolution ultra-flat haul off allows the production of films with improved flatness, and ensures better printability and lamination capability.”
  It says that the Evo Ultra Flat film flattening system works because of its position within the process. Usually, flattening systems sit just in front of the winder, but Evo Ultra Flat is installed exactly where optimum processing conditions are available for flattening the web - which means upstream, between the haul-off nip roll assembly and turner bar system.
  This arrangement offers several advantages: the film still has a temperature above 50°C, so is not fully crystallized; stretching the film is easier while it is still flexible; and the process is more energy efficient than similar systems used at the end of the process.
  Overall, flatness of lamination and barrier films can be improved by up to 40%, while sagging of the web can be reduced by up to 90%.
